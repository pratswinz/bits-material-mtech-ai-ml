0 Prologue

Every matrix is a verb, not a noun

A matrix doesn't just store numbers — it does something. Feed it a vector x and it hands back a new vector Ax: space gets stretched, squeezed, rotated, sheared, or reflected. This whole journey asks one deep question about that action.

Are there special directions that the transformation leaves alone — merely scaling them?

Those special directions are eigenvectors; the amount they stretch is the eigenvalue. To find and understand them, we first need two single-number "fingerprints" of a matrix — the determinant (how much it scales space) and the trace (a hidden sum) — and then a tool to hunt the eigenvalues down: the characteristic equation. Everything in this page connects back to that one question. Let's build it up, one interactive idea at a time.

1 The Determinant

How much does a transformation stretch space?

Picture the humble unit square sitting at the origin. Apply a matrix A and it warps into a parallelogram. The determinant is a single number that answers: by what factor did the area change — and did space get flipped inside-out?

The one-number summary

For a transformation A acting on 2D (or 3D):

  • |det(A)| = the factor by which area (2D) or volume (3D) scales. det = 2 ⇒ area doubles; det = 0.5 ⇒ it halves.
  • det(A) = 0 is the alarm bell: space is squashed into a lower dimension (a line or a point). The matrix is singular — non-invertible — because information is destroyed.
  • The sign of det(A) tells you orientation: positive keeps it, negative flips space like a mirror.

But how do we actually compute it? Minors & cofactors

For anything bigger than 2×2 we expand recursively. Strike out a row and a column, take the determinant of what's left (the minor Mij), and attach a checkerboard sign to get the cofactor Cij=(-1)i+jMij.

det(A)=∑j=1n aij Cij (expand along any row i — or any column)

The link we'll need later

An n× n matrix has full rank (its columns are linearly independent) if and only if det(A)≠ 0. Gaussian elimination reduces A to an upper-triangular U, and det(A)=(-1)sdet(U)=±∏ Uii — the product of the pivots. No zero pivots ⇔ no collapse ⇔ invertible. Hold onto this: eigenvalues are born exactly where a cleverly-shifted matrix becomes singular.

One number told us about volume. The next tells us about a hidden sum.
2 The Trace

The quiet sum along the diagonal

The trace looks almost too simple to matter: just add up the diagonal entries, tr(A)=∑i aii. Yet it carries a secret we'll cash in soon — it equals the sum of the eigenvalues. It's the determinant's understated sibling.

See the diagonal light up
tr(A)=a11+a22+a33—
Properties (all easy to prove)
tr(A+B)=tr(A)+tr(B)
tr(α A)=α tr(A)
tr(In)=n
tr(AB)=tr(BA)

That last "cyclic" property — tr(AB)=tr(BA) — is the workhorse behind countless machine-learning proofs (think gradients of tr(XTAX)).

Why the trace secretly knows the eigenvalues

The intuition. Every matrix carries a "hidden polynomial," its characteristic polynomial pA(λ)=det(A-λ I), whose roots are the eigenvalues. A polynomial is completely pinned down by its coefficients — and it turns out the two easiest coefficients to read are exactly the trace and the determinant.

The math. Expanding the determinant for any n× n matrix gives

pA(λ)=(-1)nλn+(-1)n-1tr(A) λ n-1+…+det(A).

The leading wiggle of the polynomial is set by tr(A), and its value at λ=0 is just pA(0)=det(A). Because a degree-n polynomial also equals ∏i(λi-λ) through its roots, matching the two coefficients gives a pair of beautifully simple identities:

tr(A)=∑iλi and det(A)=∏iλi.

What it means. The sum of the eigenvalues is the trace; their product is the determinant — two numbers you can read straight off the matrix at a glance. For a 2×2 matrix this is especially handy: the whole characteristic polynomial is simply λ2-tr(A) λ+det(A), so a quick mental "sum and product" already tells you the eigenvalues. We prove this carefully in Chapter 4 and use it constantly thereafter.

Now the star of the show: the directions a matrix can't turn.
3 Eigenvectors & Eigenvalues

The directions that refuse to bend

Most vectors get knocked off course by a transformation. But a precious few — the eigenvectors — come out pointing along the very same line they went in. They only get scaled, by a factor λ called the eigenvalue. They are the "grain" of the wood, the natural axes of the matrix.

Ax=λx (x≠0)

"Transforming x with the whole matrix A does nothing more than stretching it by a single number λ."

Three everyday pictures
  • Spinning globe. The rotation axis (the line through the poles) doesn't move — it's an eigenvector with λ=1. Every city off the axis swings around.
  • Stretching dough. Pull dough along one direction and squeeze another: the pull-direction is an eigenvector with λ>1, the squeeze-direction an eigenvector with 0<λ<1.
  • Shearing a deck of cards. Push the top of the deck sideways. Horizontal vectors stay horizontal (λ=1); everything else tilts. (Try the Shear preset — it has only one eigen-direction!)
A puzzle worth pausing on

Ax=λx says the transformation only rescales x along its own line. So what about a pure rotation? It turns every real direction — nothing stays put. Select the Rotation preset and try to find an eigenvector: you can't. The resolution is beautiful, and it lives in Chapter 7 — the eigenvalues become complex.

4 The Characteristic Equation

Turning a geometry problem into algebra

Dragging is fun, but we need a method. Here's the clever move that converts "find the special directions" into "find the roots of a polynomial."

Start from the definition and gather everything on one side:

Ax=λx
Ax-λ Ix=0
(A-λ I)x=0

We need a non-zero x to solve this. A square matrix sends a non-zero vector to 0 only when it collapses space — i.e. only when it is singular. And we already learned the test for that:

The bridge back to Chapter 1

A non-trivial eigenvector exists ⇔ (A-λ I) is singular ⇔

det(A-λ I)=0

Expanding this determinant gives a degree-n polynomial in λ — the characteristic polynomial. Its roots are exactly the eigenvalues. The determinant from Chapter 1 was the key all along.

The full recipe
  1. Form det(A-λ I)=0 and expand to the characteristic polynomial.
  2. Solve for its roots λ1,λ2,… — these are the eigenvalues.
  3. For each λ, find the null space of (A-λ I) by Gaussian elimination — that's the eigenspace Eλ, and any non-zero vector in it is an eigenvector.

The set of all eigenvalues is the matrix's spectrum. Worked example with A=1652: roots λ=7,-4; eigenvectors 11 and 6-5.

A delightful shortcut

If A is triangular (upper or lower), then A-λ I is triangular too, so its determinant is just the product of the diagonal: (a11-λ)(a22-λ)⋯(ann-λ)=0. The eigenvalues are simply the diagonal entries. No work required.

For one special, common family of matrices, everything becomes perfect.
5 The Spectral Theorem

The crown jewel of symmetric matrices

A matrix is symmetric when A=AT — it mirrors across its diagonal. These matrices are everywhere in data science (every covariance matrix, every ATA Gram matrix is symmetric), and they are blessed with a near-magical structure.

The Spectral Theorem (real symmetric A)

If A∈ℝn× n is symmetric, then:

  1. All eigenvalues λ are real — never complex.
  2. Eigenvectors of distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal (perpendicular).
  3. There is an orthonormal basis of ℝn made entirely of eigenvectors of A. So A is always diagonalizable — beautifully, by an orthogonal matrix.

Why symmetry forces "real" and "perpendicular"

The intuition. A symmetric matrix has no "twist" to it — it never secretly rotates space, it only stretches along some set of clean axes. Two consequences follow, and each has a short, satisfying proof.

1 · Eigenvalues are real. Start from Ax=λx and pre-multiply by the conjugate-transpose xH:

xHAx=λ xHx.

The right side has xHx=‖x‖2, which is real and positive. The left side equals its own conjugate (precisely because A=AH), so it too is real. A real number divided by a real positive number is real — therefore λ is real. Symmetric matrices simply cannot have complex eigenvalues.

2 · Eigenvectors of different eigenvalues are perpendicular. Take Ax=λx and Ay=μy with λ≠μ. Computing yHAx two ways gives λ yHx=μ yHx, so (λ-μ) yHx=0. Since λ≠μ, we must have yHx=0 — the eigenvectors meet at a right angle.

The payoff. A symmetric transformation comes with a perfect set of perpendicular axes along which it does nothing but stretch — never rotate, never shear. Drag the vector in the panel: the gold Ax always behaves as a pure stretch relative to those clean perpendicular eigen-axes. This is exactly the structure the eigendecomposition A=QΛ QT captures next.

6 Eigendecomposition

Three simple moves hidden inside one matrix

Because a symmetric matrix has perpendicular eigen-axes, we can factor it. Any real symmetric A splits as

A=Q Λ QT

where Q's columns are the orthonormal eigenvectors and Λ is the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues. Read right-to-left, applying A to a vector is really three clean steps.

7 Complex Vectors & Hermitian Matrices

When the eigenvalues leave the real line

Remember the rotation puzzle: a 90° rotation turns every real vector, so it has no real eigenvectors. The fix isn't to give up — it's to allow λ to be complex.

The rotation matrix Rθ=cosθ-sinθsinθcosθ has characteristic polynomial λ2-2cosθ λ+1, whose roots are

λ=cosθ± isinθ=e± iθ.

Unless θ=0 or π (the trivial I or -I), these are genuinely complex — and they always arrive in conjugate pairs. Geometrically, a complex eigenvalue re± iθ encodes a rotation by θ combined with scaling by r. Slide θ and watch the eigenvalues ride around the unit circle.

Eigenvaluese^{±i45°}
Rescuing "length" for complex vectors

With complex entries, the old xTx stops being a sensible length (for x=(1+i, 2+i) it gives 6+3i — not even real!). The repair is to conjugate one copy: the conjugate-transpose xH=xT. Then xHx=x1x1+…+xnxn=|x1|2+…+|xn|2=‖x‖2≥ 0, a real, non-negative number — exactly what a squared length should be.

Hermitian = symmetric, upgraded

A matrix is Hermitian if it equals its own conjugate-transpose, AH=A (the complex generalisation of AT=A). For example A=13-i3+i4 satisfies AH=A. The Spectral Theorem extends perfectly: Hermitian matrices have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors — which is precisely why quantum mechanics models every physical observable (energy, momentum, spin) as a Hermitian operator: measured values must be real.

One more decomposition — the one that lets us manufacture random data.
8 The Cholesky Decomposition

The "square root" of a symmetric matrix

If a symmetric matrix is also positive-definite (all eigenvalues positive), it has an especially tidy factorisation — into a lower-triangular matrix times its own transpose:

A=L LT (L lower-triangular, positive diagonal)

You solve for L entry-by-entry, top-left to bottom-right:

ℓ11=√(a11), ℓ21=a21ℓ11, ℓ22=√(a22-ℓ212), …

Each diagonal entry is a genuine square root — which only stays real when the matrix is positive-definite. So Cholesky doubles as a positive-definiteness test: if it runs without hitting a negative square root, the matrix is positive-definite.

The killer application: sampling a Gaussian

A multivariate Gaussian is governed by a symmetric, positive-definite covariance matrix Σ. To draw a correlated sample:

  1. Factor Σ=LLT.
  2. Draw an easy uncorrelated standard-normal vector z (a round blob).
  3. Compute x=Lz — now x has exactly covariance Σ.

L is the lens that bends a featureless round cloud into the correlated ellipse your data actually has.

9 Why It All Matters

From a clock-tower lecture to the engines of data science

Every thread we pulled — determinant, trace, eigenvectors, the Spectral Theorem, Cholesky — converges on the techniques that quietly run modern machine learning.

PCA

The eigenvectors of the covariance matrix are the principal components — directions of maximum variance. Keep the few with the largest eigenvalues and you compress data while losing almost nothing.

SVD

Built from the eigenvectors of ATA and AAT (always symmetric, always nicely behaved). It generalises eigendecomposition to any matrix — the backbone of recommender systems and compression.

Dynamical systems

Solutions of dydt=Ay look like eλ tv. The signs of the eigenvalues decide whether a system settles, oscillates, or blows up.

Quantum mechanics

Observables are Hermitian operators; their real eigenvalues are the possible measurements and the eigenvectors are the states. The Spectral Theorem makes physics consistent.

Quadratic forms & ellipsoids

xTAx=c traces an ellipsoid whose axes are A's eigenvectors and whose radii shrink with √(λ). This shapes optimisation landscapes and confidence regions.

Sampling & simulation

Cholesky's Σ=LLT turns independent noise into correlated samples — the standard trick behind Monte-Carlo finance, Gaussian processes, and synthetic data.

The single sentence to remember

A matrix transforms space; the determinant measures how much it scales volume and whether it's reversible; the trace and determinant are the sum and product of the eigenvalues; the characteristic equation det(A-λ I)=0 hunts those eigenvalues down; the eigenvectors are the directions left untouched; and for symmetric matrices the Spectral Theorem hands us a perfect set of perpendicular eigen-axes — the decomposition A=QΛ QT that powers it all.
