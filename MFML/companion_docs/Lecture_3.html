Geometry isn't given.
It's a choice you make.

How does Netflix know two movies are "close"? How does a model know two data points are "similar"? Underneath every answer is one quiet decision — how we measure length, distance, and angle. In this chapter we take apart the ruler we've always used, discover it's just one choice among many, and learn to build our own. Lengths, inner products, orthogonality, and the Gram–Schmidt process — we'll see all of it.

Chapter 1 · The ruler

First question: how long is an arrow?

Before we can talk about angles or similarity, we have to answer something simpler: how long is an arrow? A norm (written ‖x‖) is just a rule that gives every vector a length. The obvious rule is the straight-line Euclidean length — a ruler laid from tail to tip. But it isn't the only one. The Manhattan length measures distance the way a taxi drives through city blocks: only along the grid, never cutting diagonally. Drag the blue point and watch the two rulers disagree. Then switch to unit balls — every vector whose length is exactly 1. Straight-line length traces a circle; taxi length traces a diamond.
drag the blue point ↻
Intuition

A norm just has to behave like a sensible length: scaling a vector scales its length (absolute homogeneity), a detour is never shorter than the direct route (triangle inequality), and only the zero vector has zero length (positive definiteness). Many functions satisfy this — so "length" is already a choice.

Chapter 2 · The standard ruler

The dot product measures alignment.

The dot product is the single most useful operation in all of machine learning. The recipe is easy — multiply matching coordinates and add them up: x · y = x₁y₁ + x₂y₂ + …. But the real magic is what that number means: it senses how two arrows line up. It's positive when they point the same way, negative when they point opposite ways, and exactly zero when they meet at a right angle. Drag either arrow. The faint teal bar is the shadow (projection) of x onto y — the dot product is just the length of that shadow, scaled by how long y is.

Chapter 3 · Reverse-engineering the ruler

What makes the dot product work?

Here's the creative leap. Instead of accepting the dot product as handed down, let's ask which properties give it its power. It turns out there are exactly three. If we treat them not as observations but as a blueprint, any operation that obeys them becomes a brand-new, valid ruler.
RULE 1
Linearity

You can scale and add the vectors before or after taking the dot product — same answer either way.

(ax + by) · z = a(x·z) + b(y·z)

Like a bill: total the items then add tax, or tax each item then total — you pay the same.

RULE 2
Symmetry

The order doesn't matter:

x · y = y · x

"How similar is x to y" is the same question as "how similar is y to x".

RULE 3
Positive-definiteness

A vector dotted with itself is never negative, and hits zero only for the zero vector:

x · x ≥ 0,  and = 0 only if x = 0

That has to be true: x·x is the squared length, and nothing real has negative length.

The big idea

These three rules are the "rules of the game." Anything that satisfies them deserves to be called an inner product — and comes with its own notion of length, distance, and angle.

Chapter 4 · A new toolkit

Promote the blueprint to a definition.

Any operation that follows those same three rules earns a name: an inner product, written with angle brackets ⟨x, y⟩. The dot product is just the most famous member of a whole family. A space that has picked an inner product is called an inner product space — a space that knows how to measure its own geometry.
Definition · Inner product
An inner product ⟨x, y⟩ takes two vectors and returns one number, and it must obey three rules: (1) linear in each argument, (2) symmetric — ⟨x,y⟩ = ⟨y,x⟩, and (3) positive-definite — ⟨x,x⟩ > 0 for every non-zero x. Same blueprint, brand-new tools.

This raises the practical question that drives the rest of the chapter: if the dot product isn't the only inner product, how do we build the others?

Chapter 5 · The engine room

Every inner product hides a matrix.

Here's the secret. The dot product can be written with a matrix tucked in the middle — and that matrix is the identity I: x · y = xᵀ I y. Think of I as the engine quietly powering the dot product. Swap in a different matrix A and you get a brand-new inner product ⟨x,y⟩A = xᵀ A y — but only if A is the right kind of matrix.
Theorem · The master recipe
⟨x,y⟩A = xᵀ A y is a valid inner product exactly when A is symmetric and positive-definite (SPD). In other words, choosing the matrix A is choosing a geometry.
Worked check

Take x = (1, 2) and y = (3, 4). The plain dot product is (1)(3) + (2)(4) = 11. Now run it through the engine: xᵀ I y = [12] [1001] [34] = 11. Same answer — the identity really is the engine.

Chapter 6 · The moment it clicks

A new inner product reshapes space.

So what does the matrix A actually do? It reshapes our sense of distance. The "unit ball" — every vector of length exactly 1 — is a perfect circle under the dot product. Chapter 7 · The guardrail

Break the rule and length goes imaginary.

Why insist that A be positive-definite? Watch what breaks if we don't. Take the symmetric but not positive-definite matrix [100−1] and the vector x = (0, 1). Now ⟨x,x⟩ = −1, so its "length" would be √(−1) = i — imaginary. The unit set stops being a closed ellipse and tears open into a hyperbola: "near" and "far" stop meaning anything. Toggle between a healthy and a broken matrix and watch geometry fall apart.
The takeaway

Positive-definiteness is the rule that keeps length real and non-negative. It's what guarantees the unit set stays a closed ellipse — a sensible geometry — instead of an open hyperbola where distance loses all meaning.

Chapter 8 · What the new ruler gives us

One inner product, a whole toolkit.

Pick any inner product and you instantly inherit length, distance, and angle — a whole toolkit, for free. The induced norm defines length as ‖x‖ = √⟨x,x⟩, and the distance between two points is just the length of the arrow connecting them.
NORM
Induced length
‖x‖ = √⟨x,x⟩

Every inner product hands you a length. (The reverse isn't true — the taxi-cab Manhattan length doesn't come from any inner product.)

METRIC
Distance
d(x,y) = ‖x − y‖

Measure the length of the gap between them. It's never negative, ignores order, and a detour is never shorter than going direct. With the dot product, this is plain Euclidean distance.

Don't confuse them

Inner product and distance pull in opposite directions. When two vectors sit close together, their inner product is large but the distance between them is small. High inner product = high similarity = tiny gap.

Chapter 9 · Why angles even exist

The inequality that traps the ratio.

To turn that ratio into an angle, we first need one promise: the ratio ⟨x,y⟩‖x‖ ‖y‖ can never leave the range −1 to 1. That promise is the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality: |⟨x,y⟩| ≤ ‖x‖ ‖y‖. Here's the proof, made visual: slide α and watch the length of u − αv. It can never dip below zero — and the α that makes it shortest squeezes the inequality out exactly.
1.20
The proof in one line

A length squared is never negative, so ‖u − αv‖² ≥ 0 for every α. Plug in the α that makes it smallest, α = (u·v) / (v·v), and out drops (u·u)(v·v) ≥ (u·v)². It works for any inner product, not just the dot product.

Chapter 10 · The payoff

Now we can measure angle.

Because that ratio is trapped between −1 and 1, it lands in exactly the range of a cosine. So we simply define the angle ω between two vectors: cos ω = ⟨x,y⟩‖x‖ ‖y‖ One case matters more than any other in data science: when ⟨x,y⟩ = 0, the angle is 90° and the vectors are orthogonal (written x ⊥ y). Chapter 11 · A mind-bender

Perpendicular is in the eye of the geometry.

The same two arrows can be perpendicular in one geometry and not in another. Take x = (1, 1) and y = (−1, 1). Under the dot product, ⟨x,y⟩ = 0 — a perfect right angle. But switch to the geometry of [2001] and those same arrows give ⟨x,y⟩ = −1, an angle of about 109°. Same arrows, different right angle. Toggle the geometry and watch the angle change.
Chapter 12 · The transformations that change nothing

Rotations preserve every length and angle.

A square matrix is orthogonal when its columns are orthonormal — equivalently, when AᵀA = I, so its transpose is its inverse (Aᵀ = A⁻¹). These are the rigid motions: rotations and reflections. Apply one and ‖Ax‖ = ‖x‖ — every length is left untouched, and the angle between any two vectors is preserved exactly. Spin the rotation below: the shape turns, but it never stretches or squishes.

Chapter 13 · Food for thought

In high dimensions, random vectors are perpendicular.

Here's a strange and important fact. Pick two vectors at random (each coordinate an independent Gaussian) and measure the angle between them. In 2D the angle is all over the place. But as the dimension grows, the angle concentrates sharply around 90° — almost every pair of random high-dimensional vectors is nearly orthogonal. Crank up the dimension and sample thousands of pairs.
2
Why it matters

This "concentration" is everywhere in machine learning: in high dimensions, distances flatten out and almost everything looks equidistant and orthogonal — the famous curse of dimensionality. It's also a blessing: near-orthogonality lets us pack many almost-independent directions into one space.

Chapter 14 · The ideal coordinate system

The dream basis: unit length, mutually perpendicular.

A basis only needs to be linearly independent — its vectors can be any lengths, at any (non-parallel) angles. But the nicest basis is an orthonormal one: every vector has length 1, and every pair is orthogonal. Then coordinates are trivial to read off — just project. Any rotation of the standard axes is orthonormal. Spin it and see a clean, rigid grid.

Definition · Orthonormal basis
Basis vectors b₁, …, bₙ form an orthonormal basis when each has length 1 and every pair is orthogonal — in symbols, ⟨bi, bj⟩ = 1 when i = j, and 0 otherwise. (Perpendicular but not unit length? Then it's only an orthogonal basis.)
Chapter 15 · Building the perfect grid

Gram–Schmidt: subtract the shadow.

Given any "messy" basis, how do we manufacture an orthonormal one? The Gram–Schmidt process does it one vector at a time. Keep the first vector (just normalize it). For the second, remove the part that lies along the first — subtract its shadow (projection) — and what remains is perpendicular. Normalize, and repeat. Watch the construction step by step: the gray projection is the shadow we subtract away.

Chapter 16 · Factor a matrix · LU

LU: Gaussian elimination, bottled.

Just as a number factors into pieces (12 = 4 × 3), a matrix factors too — and the first factorization falls straight out of something you already do. When you row-reduce A to its row-echelon form (REF), the result is upper-triangular; call it U. The one new idea: keep the receipt. Each row operation — "subtract m times one row from another" — is itself a matrix, an elementary matrix E. Collect every multiplier you used into a lower-triangular L, and you've recorded the whole elimination. The result is A = L U: U is the REF, L is how you got there. Step through a symmetric, positive-definite A and watch each row op become an elementary matrix.
How to build it · why it pays off

Make one elementary matrix per row operation (an identity with a single multiplier placed below the diagonal). Multiplying them in order turns A into U; inverting that product collects the multipliers into L. The payoff: once A = L U, solving Ax = b is two quick triangular slides — L y = b top-down, then U x = y bottom-up — with no re-eliminating. To check your work, just multiply L and U back together and confirm you land on A.

Chapter 17 · Factor a matrix · Cholesky

Cholesky: the square root of a matrix.

Run that same elimination on a matrix that is symmetric and positive-definite, and something tidy happens: the receipt L and the result U are mirror images, so you can fold them into a single lower triangle. That gives A = L Lᵀ — the matrix version of a square root (just like √9 = 3 with 3 × 3 = 9). One triangle, used twice: half the storage, half the work of LU, and it exists only for SPD matrices.
The "aha"

Cholesky is just LU with the symmetry spent. Look at the pivots elimination left on U's diagonal — 4, 2, 1.5. Cholesky's diagonal is exactly their square roots — 2, √2, √1.5. And those roots stay real and positive for one reason: A is positive-definite, so nothing negative ever sits under the root — the very same guardrail from Chapter 7. Break it and Cholesky simply can't exist.

Chapter 18 · Factor a matrix · QR

QR: straighten the columns.

The last factorization comes from the other elimination you learned — Gram–Schmidt (Chapter 15), which straightens the columns instead of zeroing them out. For any tall matrix A (size m × n with m > n) whose columns are independent, QR writes A = Q R: Q holds the orthonormal columns Gram–Schmidt produces (clean, perpendicular, unit-length directions), and R is the bookkeeping that rebuilds each original column from those clean ones. Since the k-th clean direction uses only columns 1…k, R comes out upper-triangular — the same staircase shape as a REF.
Notice the diagonal of R

Each diagonal entry of R is the length of the leftover perpendicular piece Gram–Schmidt carved out for that column (√2 and 1/√2 here) — and it's always positive. That's the tell: if a column were a blend of the earlier ones, its leftover would collapse to length 0 and drop a zero onto R's diagonal. So a strictly positive diagonal is a certificate that the columns are linearly independent. For a random tall matrix — say 7 × 5 with independent columns — you'll get a 7 × 5 Q with orthonormal columns and a 5 × 5 upper-triangular R whose diagonal entries are all non-zero.

The big picture

Geometry is a lens you choose.

We started by asking what "close" means and discovered the dot product is just one ruler among many. We reverse-engineered its three rules into the inner product, learned that a symmetric positive-definite matrix is the engine that reshapes space, and saw how a single choice of A bends the unit circle into an ellipse — redefining length, distance, angle, and even what "perpendicular" means. Finally, the same two moves — elimination and orthogonalization — became the factorizations LU, Cholesky, and QR that turn all of this into something a computer can actually run. The math doesn't hand you a geometry. It hands you the freedom to design one.
