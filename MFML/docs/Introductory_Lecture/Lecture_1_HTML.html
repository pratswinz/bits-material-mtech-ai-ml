isn't equations.
It's the geometry of thought.

Chapter 1 · Meet v

Our story begins with a single arrow.

Before matrices, before equations, before machine learning — there is one object. We call it v. And the first thing to know is: v is three things at once. An arrow. A list of numbers. A recipe of basis vectors. Drag the tip — and watch all three views update in lockstep. They are not three things; they are one thing seen from three angles.

Chapter 2 · Now do something to it

One number. Stretches, squishes, flips.

We have v. The simplest possible thing we can do to it is multiply by a number — a scalar λ. Direction never changes. Only length stretches, squishes, or flips through the origin. Notice the dotted line: every λ·v lives on it. This line is the span of v — the entire universe one vector and one number can reach. To break free of this line, we'll need a second arrow.

Chapter 3 · Bring in a second arrow

Two arrows. One walk.

A second vector w lets us escape the line. Place v and w tip-to-tail and the total displacement is v + w — the gold arrow. Notice that the parallelogram closes either way: v→w lands at the same point as w→v. Addition is commutative. But the deeper observation: adding two vectors gives a vector. Scaling gives a vector. You cannot escape the space using its own operations. That property has a name — closure — and it is the entire definition of a vector space.
drag v or w ↻
Chapter 4 · Now combine the two moves

From two arrows, the entire plane.

We've learned to scale (Chapter 2) and to add (Chapter 3). Combine both: αv + βw. That's a linear combination. Hit "Paint reachable points" and watch every α-β pair fill the plane with reachable destinations. Two arrows generated all of 2D. But — toggle to "Dependent" — when v and w point along the same line, the painted plane collapses back to a single line. Two arrows that don't add information. That is linear dependence, made visible.
Chapter 5 · Step out of the page

Everything we've said scales up.

Two arrows gave us a plane. Add a third, and we get all of 3D. The story doesn't change — we just have one more basis direction (k̂), and one more number per vector. Linear algebra doesn't care about dimension. The same closure, the same span, the same independence. And that is why the moment you understand 2D, you also understand ℝ⁷⁸⁴ — where a single MNIST image lives as one point.

Chapter 6 · Now bundle the arrows

Two vectors, side by side. That's a matrix.

We've been working with vectors one at a time. Now bundle two of them into a single object — write them as columns next to each other — and you have a matrix. But here is the reframe that changes everything: each column isn't just data. It says "this is where one basis vector lands." Slide the controls and watch — the red arrow always shows where î goes; the yellow always shows where ĵ goes. The grid follows. A matrix is a packed instruction for moving the basis vectors — and once those move, the whole space moves with them.

Chapter 7 · Pause — why does this matter?

Your photo. Your dataset. Your model. All matrices.

Before we discover what matrices do, let's see what they store. An image — a matrix of pixel intensities. A dataset — a matrix of features. Every neural network layer — a matrix of weights. The reason we are spending today on this is simple: in ML, everything you touch is a matrix or a vector. Linear algebra isn't a topic in machine learning — it's the carrier wave. Chapter 8 · The moment everything clicks

A matrix moves space.

Chapter 6 told us a matrix says where the basis vectors land. Watch what that means. The left half is input space — the home of x. The right half is output space — and as A is applied, the entire grid warps. Every green dot you see takes a journey to its destination. Chapter 9 · Now flip the question

Given the destination, find the start.

In Chapter 8, A took an x and produced Ax. Now reverse it: "I see where I landed — call it b. What x got me there?"   That is Ax = b. And there are two beautiful ways to see the same answer.

Row picture (left): each equation is a line. The solution is where the lines cross.
Column picture (right): b is built by stacking copies of A's columns. The solution tells us how many of each.

Hit play and watch both pictures find the answer simultaneously.
Chapter 10 · But does a solution always exist?

Every system ends in exactly three ways.

In Chapter 9 our journey landed cleanly on b. But what if no x exists that gets us there? What if many do? It turns out — no matter how complicated a system is — it can only end in one of three geometric fates. One. None. Or infinite.   Look at the lines. When they cross — one solution. When they're parallel — none. When they're the same line — infinite. The geometry tells you everything; the algebra is just bookkeeping.
Chapter 11 · So how do we tell which fate?

Measure how much space stretched.

In Chapter 10 we saw that "no solution" and "infinite solutions" share something — both happen when A has crushed a dimension. We need a single number that detects this collapse. Take the unit square — area 1. Apply A. The new area is det(A). When det > 0, space stretched cleanly. When det < 0, space flipped through its mirror. When det = 0, a dimension just died — and Chapter 14's "undo button" will not exist.

Chapter 12 · And in 3D?

Same number. Now it's volume.

Just as Chapter 5 told us our 2D rules carry to higher dimensions, so does the determinant. In 2D it measured area. In 3D it measures the volume of the parallelepiped formed by the three columns of A. Det = 0 still means collapse — only now the three columns lie in a flat plane instead of filling space. Same intuition, one dimension up.

Chapter 13 · A small but important detour

Flip rows and columns. That's a transpose.

Before our final question, one quick move you'll see everywhere. Take A. Write its rows as columns instead. The new matrix is Aᵀ. Geometrically: a reflection across the main diagonal. The cell at (i,j) ends up at (j,i). And one fact worth remembering: (AB)ᵀ = BᵀAᵀ — the order reverses, like undoing socks-then-shoes. We'll need this thinking for Chapter 14.
Chapter 14 · The final question

Can we undo a matrix?

Our journey closes here. A moved space (Chapter 8). Ax = b asked us to rewind it (Chapter 9). The determinant told us when rewinding is possible (Chapter 11). Now we make it explicit: the matrix that perfectly reverses A is its inverse, A⁻¹. Apply A. Apply A⁻¹. The grid lands precisely where it started. Nothing changed. Information was preserved. But — when det(A) = 0, a dimension was destroyed in the squash, and no inverse exists. The undo button is gray. Hit play and watch the round trip.
Chapter 15 · Elementary row operations

Three legal moves. Solution set never changes.

In Chapter 9 we said "find x such that Ax = b" — but how, when guessing fails? Gaussian elimination. Three elementary row operations on the augmented matrix [A | b]:

① Swap two rows  (Ri ↔ Rj)
② Multiply a row by non-zero λ  (Ri ← λ·Ri, λ ≠ 0)
③ Add a multiple of one row to another  (Ri ← Ri + λ·Rj)

Each move rewrites the equations into a simpler form, but the solution set is preserved exactly. The intersection point (2, 1) below never moves — every legal step keeps it pinned.
Chapter 16 · Row-echelon form

The pivots fall in a staircase.

After enough row ops, the matrix takes a special shape — row-echelon form (REF). The first non-zero entry of each row is called the pivot. Rule: each pivot must lie strictly to the right of the pivot in the row above. That single rule produces a staircase pattern descending from upper-left to lower-right. Pivot columns own basic variables; the others are free variables.
Chapter 17 · Reduced row-echelon form

Take REF further. Each pivot becomes 1, alone in its column.

REF was good. Reduced row-echelon form (RREF) is canonical. Two extra rules: every pivot equals 1, and every pivot column has only zeros above and below the pivot. The pivot columns become standard basis vectors of ℝᵐ. Once a system is in RREF, the answer is sitting on the right side of the bar — no back-substitution needed.
Chapter 18 · Particular & general solutions

One answer. Plus all the ways to wiggle.

When a system has free variables, infinitely many x satisfy Ax = b — but every one of them has the same recipe: a particular solution (one specific x that works), plus any combination of solutions to Ax = 0 (the wiggle room).

The example from the slide deck — a 2×4 system in RREF: [1 0 8 −4 | 42] · [0 1 2 12 | 8]. Two pivots, two free variables. Watch the particular solution emerge by setting free vars to zero — then watch it wiggle.
